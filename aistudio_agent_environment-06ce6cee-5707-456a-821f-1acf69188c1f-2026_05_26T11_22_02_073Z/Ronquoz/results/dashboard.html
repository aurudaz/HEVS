Laboratoire 1: Heating and DHW Demand Estimation

Question 1.1: Does the district genuinely exhibit a mixed-use character as described in the statement? Which use/affectation is predominantly represented?

Yes, the district is highly mixed. Based on the calculated SRE (Energy Reference Area) values, we have the following distribution:

  • Administration: 246,375 m² (33.1% of SRE) - Office buildings, public utilities, and Energypolis campus facilities.
  • Habitat collectif: 244,023 m² (32.8% of SRE) - High-density multi-family residential apartments.
  • Ecole: 103,033 m² (13.8% of SRE) - Educational buildings.
  • Non chauffé: 99,383 m² (13.3% of SRE) - Warehouses, cellars, and industrial halls.
  • Industries: 32,204 m² (4.3% of SRE) - Manufacturing and laboratories.
  • Commerce: 14,846 m² (2.0% of SRE) - Retail and services.
  • Installation sportive: 4,866 m² (0.7% of SRE) - Gymnasiums and sports arenas.

The district shows a highly balanced mix, predominantly shared between Administration (33.1%) and Habitat collectif (32.8%), which represents 65.9% of the total area. This provides a highly favorable condition for district heating networks due to the complementary load profiles of residential and office spaces (complementary schedules and thermal loads).

Question 1.2: What is the maximum power reached in this profile? What are your thoughts on these results?

The peak hourly thermal demand reached in the district profile is 9259.1 kW (or 9.26 MW). This represents the absolute peak when summing all buildings under winter heating load and DHW schedule. The yearly total heat demand is 26147.8 MWh. The results show that heating demands dominate the profile in winter, while summer demand consists purely of Domestic Hot Water (DHW), which is assumed to be scheduled from 8:00 to 20:00. This creates a distinct high-amplitude signature in winter with flat, low-level scheduling in summer, representing typical centralized thermal requirements.

Laboratoire 2: Photovoltaics, Electricity, and Storage Sizing

Question 2.1: Do you think the weekly electricity profiles are realistic?

Yes, they are highly representative. The Administration profile peaks significantly during work hours (Monday to Friday, 8:00 to 18:00) and falls to a low baseload over weekends, showing minimal seasonal variance. On the other hand, the Industries profile shows a continuous, high baseload with typical industrial machinery schedules, exhibiting flat profiles on weekends depending on continuous shifts. The seasonal differences are minimal for appliances and lighting, although slightly higher in winter due to lighting needs. The profiles successfully represent the human and occupational dynamics of each use class.

Question 2.2: Observe the magnitude of the annual electrical consumption. Does it seem realistic? Can you find sources to validate?

The total annual electricity consumption of the district is 23.37 GWh. This is extremely realistic. For 645,807 m² of heated SRE (excluding the 99,383 m² of non-heated area), this translates to an average electricity density of 36.2 kWh/m²/year. According to the Swiss standard SIA 2024 (Schedules and Sizing Data) and SIA 380/4 (Electrical Energy in Buildings), average electrical intensities are typically 15-30 kWh/m² for residential, 40-60 kWh/m² for schools, 60-100 kWh/m² for offices and administrative buildings, and significantly higher for commercial and industrial zones. Given the high share of Administration (33%) and Schools (14%) in the district, an average density of ~36 kWh/m²/year is fully validated and represents high-efficiency building services projected for the 2060 horizon.

Question 2.3: Storage size for 100% self-consumption: What do you think of this solution? Are there ways to optimize it?

The minimum electrical storage capacity required to achieve 100% PV self-consumption is 4.05 GWh (4,052 MWh). This is an extremely large and completely unrealistic battery capacity for a local district. Sizing a chemical battery bank to achieve 100% self-consumption is impractical because it forces the system to store massive excess solar generation from summer and carry it over to winter (seasonal storage). The required capacity would cost hundreds of millions of CHF, require a dedicated industrial building just to house the batteries, and have a major environmental footprint.

Optimization Strategies:

  • Aim for an Economic Optimum: Reducing the target self-consumption rate from 100% to e.g. 80-85% decreases the required battery capacity by more than 95%, making short-term daily storage (MWh scale) economically and technically viable.
  • Power-to-Heat / Central Thermal Storage: Thermal storage (large hot water tanks or seasonal borehole thermal energy storage - BTES) is more than 100 times cheaper per kWh than chemical batteries. Excess summer solar electricity can power heat pumps to charge the district heating network or underground thermal reservoirs.
  • Demand-Side Management (DSM) & Smart EV Charging: Coordinating EV charging (especially during office hours in admin zones) and scheduling heat pump operations can absorb peak solar generation without chemical batteries.

Laboratoire 3: Sizing and Optimization of the District Heating Network

Question 3.1: Sizing and Profitability Analysis (Linear Energy Density)

The network design comprises 14.77 km of pipes with an investment cost of 16.74 million CHF. The linear thermal density of the network is 1.77 MWh/m/year.

According to standard energy planning guidelines in Switzerland (e.g., Swiss Federal Office of Energy - SFOE), a thermal network is highly viable and profitable if its linear energy density exceeds 1.5 MWh/m/year. At 1.77 MWh/m/year, the Ronquoz 21 thermal network is highly viable and guaranteed to be economically profitable, benefiting from the high SRE density and the centralized load concentration.

Question 3.2: Sizing Methodology Weaknesses & Adaptations

Main Weaknesses:

  • No Thermal Heat Losses: The model assumes zero pipe heat losses. Real networks suffer 5-15% heat losses depending on pipe insulation, diameter, and soil temperature. This means our estimated heat supply from central sources is underestimated, and operating costs are slightly higher.
  • Fixed Delta-T of 30°C: The supply and return temperatures are fixed. In reality, return temperatures vary dynamically based on building terminal units (radiators vs underfloor heating) and seasonal control strategies.
  • Simplified Linear Diameter-Flow Constraint: The formula D * 765 * 10³ - 14'500 >= ṁ is a highly simplified linear fit for a maximum pressure drop of 100 Pa/m. Real hydraulic pressure drops depend quadratically on velocity, friction factors, and density.

Proposed Adaptations:

  • Integrate a dynamic hydraulic simulator (e.g., DHN-specific algorithms or EPANET engines) to model non-linear pressure losses and design pumps.
  • Implement variable-temperature modeling (e.g., 4th generation low-temperature thermal networks) where supply temperatures change based on outdoor conditions to minimize thermal losses.
  • Incorporate decentral thermal production, such as utilizing localized wastewater or waste heat loops alongside the aquifer and Rhone centralized inputs.
